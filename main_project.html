18% of adults in the United States have a mental, behavioral, or emotional disorder.


Mental illness is misunderstood and underfunded. Although depression is the second most prevalent illness in the U.S. today, it ranks 68th (out of 235) in the amount of federal funding it receives. Funding must reflect these numbers in order to expand the research which provides the answers needed to fight mental health illnesses.

THE IMPORTANCE
OF RESEARCH

There is currently no medical diagnosis for a variety of mental health illnesses like depression. Diagnosis is based on subjective criteria and people suffering from these diseases can slip through the cracks even when they seek help.

Mental illnesses include depression, anxiety disorders, schizophrenia, eating disorders and addictive behaviors. They are usually not thought about as "real" diseases, such as cancer because we can't always see the pain these diseases inflict. Scientists don't understand the root cause of these issues and the lack of federal funding reflects the stagnant nature of this important research.

One in 5 adults in America experience a mental illness.


One-half of all chronic mental illness begins by the age of 14; three quarters by the age of 24.


2.4 million American's live with schizophrenia.


6.1 million American's live with bipolar disorder.


26 percent of adults staying in shelters live with serious mental illness.


Nearly 60 percent of adults with mental illness didn't recieve mental health services in the previous year.


African American and Hispanic Americans used mental health services at about 1/2 the rate of whites in the past year and Asian Americans about 1/3 the rate.


Research shows that only 50% of patients respond to existing medications to treat depression.


Since the introduction of Prozac in 1985, there have been NO new medications to treat depression.


Efforts are under way to change how depression is defined and diagnosed in research.


The World Health Organization has identified a team called Mental Health: Evidence and Research Team (MER) who is committed to closing the gap between what is needed and what is currently available to reduce the burden of mental disorders.




“Ultimately, depression is as biological as cancer and heart disease; it is simply a matter of identifying the relevant molecules."


–Eric Nestler, a psychiatrist and neuroscientist at the Icahn School of Medicine at Mount Sinai in New York City




Although depression is the second most prevalent illness in the U.S. today, it ranks 68th (out of 235) in the amount of federal funding it receives.


The US National Institutes of Health spent about $5.3 billion on cancer research in 2013 compared to only $415 million spent on depression research and $2.2 billion on mental-health research as a whole.
